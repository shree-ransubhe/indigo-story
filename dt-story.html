End-to-end experience strategy

I don't start with screens. I start with the business problem, the service architecture, and the moments that matter across channels. Here's how that played out at airline scale.

Defining the vision

From four fragmented journeys to one coherent experience

When I joined IndiGo, the booking, check-in, in-flight entertainment, and loyalty (BluChip) experiences had each evolved independently—different interaction patterns, no shared quality bar, no common design language across web, app, kiosks, and crew tools.

I defined the end-to-end experience strategy: what the passenger journey should feel like across every digital and physical touchpoint, from first search to post-flight. Then I built the roadmap to get there—staging delivery so the business could ship value continuously, not wait for a big-bang relaunch.

Multi-market complexity

Navigating regulatory, legal, and entity boundaries

IndiGo's legal team required a hard separation: the flight business and the marketplace business are distinct entities with different compliance obligations. The digital experience had to reflect that clearly—across branding, information architecture, and interaction patterns.

This is structurally the same challenge a European telecom faces across markets—different regulations, different entities, shared brand umbrella. I've navigated it hands-on, not theoretically.

Service design at scale

Phygital: not just screens, ecosystems

Aviation is inherently a service design problem. The passenger journey spans digital surfaces (web, app), physical touchpoints (airport kiosks, boarding gates), and operational systems (crew tools, catering ops, irregular operations). Designing one without understanding the others creates friction passengers feel immediately.

I led the phygital transformation across these touchpoints—mapping the end-to-end service, identifying handoff failures between digital and physical, and ensuring design decisions held up when a passenger moved from app to kiosk to gate.

Strategy in practice

Four hero journeys shipped, not theorised

Booking, check-in, IFE, BluChip loyalty—each redesigned with a common interaction model, reduced dead-end states, and a tighter funnel from search to confirmation. Shipped in stages. Result: fewer support escalations, cleaner engineering handoff, and a base that product teams can now A/B test against.

Mentoring with intent

Growing designers who outgrow their roles

I don't mentor by proximity. I give each designer a growth thesis: where they're strong, where the gap is, and what project exposure will close it. Then I create those opportunities deliberately—putting someone on a complex flow when they're ready, pairing them with engineering leads, or asking them to present to stakeholders before they think they're ready.

The result: designers who can now run programs, negotiate with product, and defend design decisions in review without me in the room.

Operating model

One team, regardless of contract type

At IndiGo, design capacity is distributed—internal pods, agency partners, time-and-material contractors. Different contracts, different rituals, different definitions of "done." I built one operating model for all of them: shared Figma libraries, a single review committee, structured onboarding that gets new members shipping in their first sprint.

This is directly transferable to building a design org in India for a European company—multiple partner types, distributed across locations, but operating with one quality bar and one culture.

Scalable design systems & governance

Design systems are governance made tangible. Here's how I built one under real constraints—legal, brand, timeline.

Marketplace Design System

4 weeks, 6 designers, legally mandated

IndiGo's legal team required a distinct visual identity for the marketplace business vs. the flight business. No existing system addressed this. I shaped the concept, validated with the CDIO and CMO, then ran it end-to-end: 4 weeks, 6 designers, token-driven, Figma-native, covering typography, colour, spacing, and component foundations.

The turnaround became a symbol of agility inside the org—and gave the mobile app revamp and future marketplace builds a ready-made foundation.

Governance that scales

Not bureaucracy—operational discipline

I established peer review gates, token-based handoff to engineering via Figma Dev Mode, and a DSM sync cadence that kept the system alive as products evolved. External partners followed the same governance—no separate standards for outsourced work.

For a multi-market telecom, this pattern scales directly: local teams contribute within a governed system, global consistency without stifling local needs.

Influencing senior stakeholders

Design influence isn't about convincing people with pretty screens. It's about building evidence systems and speaking the language of business decisions.

Design CSAT

Turning feedback into leverage

I introduced a structured post-delivery mechanism where product, business, and engineering score design quality. Not a survey for the sake of it—a forcing function to discuss what worked and what didn't while memory is fresh.

This gave me data to take to leadership when advocating for headcount, tooling investment, and process changes. Stakeholder alignment became evidence-based, not opinion-based.

C-suite alignment

CDIO, CMO, and cross-functional narratives

The Marketplace Design System required buy-in from both the CDIO (technology/digital) and CMO (brand). Different stakeholders, different concerns. I structured the pitch differently for each: technical governance for the CDIO, brand differentiation for the CMO. Both approved.

I regularly present design rationale in business terms—impact on conversion, support cost reduction, delivery velocity—because that's what earns continued investment.

Storytelling

Making design visible to the organisation

I built visibility rituals: stakeholder show-and-tells that frame design work in terms of business outcomes, a utilisation dashboard so leadership can see team capacity and throughput, and a delivery cadence that makes progress tangible week over week.

The design function went from "we'll get to it" to a predictable, respected part of the product delivery engine.

Why this maps directly to telecom

Aviation and telecom are closer than most people realise. Both are high-volume, regulated, multi-touchpoint service businesses where digital experience is the primary commerce layer. Here's the structural mapping.

  • Self-service portals — IndiGo's web check-in and account management map directly to telecom self-service: plan management, usage dashboards, SIM activation, bill pay. Same design challenge: reduce call-centre load by making complex tasks achievable without support.
  • Plan selection = Booking funnel — Searching and selecting a flight is structurally identical to choosing a telecom plan: compare options, understand add-ons, commit, pay. Same cognitive load, same conversion pressure, same need for clarity.
  • Multi-channel consistency — IndiGo spans app, web, kiosk, IFE, and crew tools. Telecom spans app, web, retail stores, call centres, and partner channels. The design system governance challenge is the same: one brand, many surfaces, consistent experience.
  • Multi-entity / multi-market compliance — I've already navigated legally mandated entity separation (flight vs. marketplace). In telecom, this maps to market-by-market regulatory differences within a shared brand umbrella—exactly the multi-market challenge this role demands.
  • Distributed team orchestration — Multiple internal pods, agency partners, TnM vendors across locations. This is how a European telecom's India design capability will operate. I've built and run this model.
  • Enterprise internal tools — I've designed crew-facing tools (COPS, IROPS/internal booking) that are direct parallels to telecom's internal platforms: network ops dashboards, retail POS systems, customer care tooling. Enterprise UX at its core.
