Context & stakes

IndiGo operates 2,000+ daily flights and serves roughly 3 lakh users every day across multiple phygital touchpoints—web, mobile, airport kiosks, in-flight screens, and crew-facing tools. At this scale, digital products behave like conversion-critical commerce flows: search, selection, purchase, and post-booking tasks must stay coherent under load, regulation, and operational change.

  • Consistency across channels and journey stages—web, app, in-flight, and on-ground touchpoints all speaking the same language.
  • Delivery cadence — predictable design throughput, review discipline, and partner alignment across a large distributed team.
  • Visibility — stakeholders across product, tech, CX, and marketing need shared language for quality, progress, and trade-offs.
  • Brand elevation — digital experience should reinforce a modern, premium-capable carrier, not just the "low cost" perception.

What I drove

Each of these started with a real problem I walked into, not a roadmap I inherited. Here's what was broken, what I did about it, and what changed.

Journeys

Four high-stakes paths, one quality bar

When I joined, the booking, check-in, IFE, and BluChip loyalty flows had grown independently—different patterns, inconsistent interaction models, no shared review gate. Passengers noticed.

I led the redesign across all four, working directly with product owners and front-end leads to set a common interaction language, reduce dead-end states, and tighten the funnel from search to confirmation. The work shipped in stages, not as a big-bang relaunch.

The result: fewer support escalations on these flows, cleaner handoff to engineering per sprint, and a base that product can now A/B test against rather than rebuilding from scratch each time.

Design ops

From chaos to a 250% faster cycle

Design delivery was unpredictable—no shared review cadence, no utilization visibility, and partners working to different quality bars. Stakeholders couldn't tell what was "in review" vs. "done."

I set up a governance model: daily DSM syncs with the team, mandatory peer reviews before dev handoff, a utilization dashboard so leads could rebalance load, and clear quality checkpoints that everyone—including external partners—followed.

Internal tracking showed a 250% improvement in design delivery turnaround. More importantly, teams stopped re-doing work because reviews caught issues early instead of late.

Systems

Marketplace Design System

IndiGo's legal team flagged a hard requirement: the flight business and the marketplace business needed a distinct visual identity—they're separate entities, and the digital experience had to reflect that clearly. No existing system addressed this split.

I shaped the Marketplace Design System concept, validated the direction first with the CDIO, then with the CMO, and ran the full revision end-to-end—4 weeks, 6 designers, token-driven, Figma-native, covering typography, color, spacing, and component foundations purpose-built to differentiate the marketplace while staying coherent with IndiGo's broader brand.

The turnaround became a symbol of agility inside the org. It also gave the mobile app revamp and future marketplace builds a ready-made foundation instead of starting from zero.

Voice of design

Closing the feedback loop

After a major delivery, feedback was anecdotal—someone in a hallway saying "looks off" or a comment buried in an unthreaded email chain or Teams chat. No structured signal, no learning.

I introduced Design CSAT: a lightweight post-delivery mechanism where product, business, and engineering score what landed and what didn't. Not a survey for the sake of it—a forcing function to talk about quality while memory is fresh.

Teams now iterate on evidence, not impressions. It also gave me data to take to leadership when advocating for design headcount and tooling investment.

Organization

Unifying a distributed team

Design capacity was scattered—internal pods, agency partners, TnM contract members—each with their own rituals, file structures, and definition of "done."

I brought everyone under one operating model: shared Figma libraries, a single review committee for major programs, and a structured onboarding track so new partners could ship confidently within their first sprint.

The payoff was compound—every new team member or partner ramped faster, and quality stopped depending on which pod happened to pick up the work.

Innovation

AI where it earns its place

Everyone was talking about AI in design, but nobody had tried it against IndiGo's actual constraints—brand governance, regulatory copy, multilingual requirements, and the pace of airline ops.

I ran focused pilots: AI-assisted exploration for layout variants, rapid synthesis of research findings, and prototype generation for internal review. Each pilot had clear success criteria and a human review gate.

What survived: practical acceleration in early-stage ideation and a playbook for where AI helps vs. where it creates risk. What got cut: anything that couldn't pass the brand and legal checkpoints.

Patterns that travel across industries

Aviation and e-commerce share more DNA than people assume. The core design challenges—high-traffic funnels, payments under pressure, identity verification, mobile-first users—are structurally the same.

  • Booking → Cart & checkout — IndiGo's booking funnel (search, select, pay, confirm) maps directly to e-commerce cart and checkout flows. Same conversion pressure, same need for clarity under cognitive load.
  • Check-in → Account & identity — Web and kiosk check-in is a verification and preference-setting journey—the same pattern as onboarding, profile management, or order status in any commerce product.
  • Payments at scale — Multi-currency, multi-gateway payment orchestration with real-time inventory pressure. IndiGo processes millions of transactions; the design considerations around trust, error recovery, and confirmation are universal.
  • Mobile app revamp — Currently leading the native app redesign (Phoenix prototype in SwiftUI, Figma token-driven). The mobile-first, performance-sensitive, personalisation-ready challenges are identical to any commerce app rebuild.
  • Design system at org scale — The Marketplace Design System serves distributed squads and external partners—same governance, adoption, and handoff challenges as any multi-team e-commerce design system.
  • AI in the workflow, not just the product — AI pilots for design acceleration, plus personal builds exploring conversational and assistant UI patterns.
